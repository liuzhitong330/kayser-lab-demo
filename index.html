Sleep Genetics Has Thousands of Loci and Knows the Biology of Almost None

The Kayser lab works on the biology of sleep — using Drosophila genetics and human genomics to understand why we sleep, what goes wrong in sleep disorders, and how the brain implements sleep circuits. Their specific strategy is compelling: take candidate genes from human sleep GWAS, find the fly ortholog, run genetic manipulations, and use the phenotype to dissect the circuit and molecular mechanism. The result is a direct path from association to mechanism that neither human genetics nor model organism work alone could provide.

I wanted to quantify the problem the Kayser lab is solving. How many GWAS loci for sleep traits are actually out there, and what fraction have any functional explanation?

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. Sleep trait associations downloaded from the GWAS Catalog (EBI), July 2026. Kayser lab validated genes curated manually from published papers (sources listed in Figure 2 caption). Locus counts aggregate all genome-wide significant associations per mapped trait; multiple studies per trait are summed.

Over 6,600 GWAS loci for sleep — and most are functionally dark

There are over 4,400 genome-wide significant insomnia loci and roughly 6,600 total across all sleep traits — yet the cellular and circuit mechanisms behind the vast majority are completely unknown. This is the core problem: GWAS excels at finding associations but cannot tell you whether a locus acts in neurons or glia, during development or in the adult, via synaptic structure or neuropeptide signaling. Translating any single locus to biology requires the kind of experimental work that Drosophila makes tractable: you can knock down a gene in a specific neuron type, measure sleep with single-animal resolution, and dissect the circuit in weeks rather than years.

The Kayser lab's pipeline: GWAS locus → fly ortholog → mechanism

CADM2/beat-Ia is one of the cleanest examples of how this pipeline works: a human GWAS hit for excessive daytime sleepiness at the CADM2 locus, fly knockdown of the ortholog beat-Ia causes sleepy flies, the mechanism turns out to be a defect in neuropeptide F (NPF) neurite elaboration that disrupts input to arousal-promoting GABAergic neurons, and an NPY receptor agonist (NPF is the fly homolog of human NPY) rescues sleep in zebrafish lacking CADM2. From association to potential therapeutic target in one paper.
The TDP-43/SIK3 finding is the most surprising: sleep disruption in neurodegeneration is driven by peripheral metabolic dysfunction, not neural circuit loss. Rodriguez et al. 2026 showed that TDP-43 expression induces a starvation-like metabolic state in the periphery, and SIK3 (a kinase also linked to sleep homeostasis in mammalian studies) suppresses both the sleep phenotype and the metabolic defect by remodeling the pentose phosphate pathway. This means that for at least one neurodegenerative disease model, the therapeutic target for sleep disruption is in the body, not the brain — a genuinely unexpected result that only a whole-organism model like Drosophila could reveal.

Connection to the lab's work

  • Cross-species GWAS validation: The lab's multi-trait clustering approach to identify sub-threshold GWAS candidates (EGR2 paper) is something I find particularly interesting — it's a way to extract more biological signal from existing genomic data without waiting for larger cohorts. I'd be excited to contribute to the computational side of that pipeline, particularly the variant-to-gene mapping and candidate prioritization steps.
  • Sleep and neurodegeneration: The SIK3 result raises the question of whether peripheral metabolic dysfunction drives sleep disruption in other neurodegenerative disease models too (alpha-synuclein? tau?). This seems like a natural extension, and one where transcriptomic and metabolomic readouts — which I've worked with computationally — would be the key tools.
  • On the experimental side, I have hands-on experience with mammalian cell culture, Western blot, ELISA, flow cytometry, fluorescence microscopy, and protein analytical methods. I've also applied RNA-seq analysis computationally. I'm comfortable contributing across both bench and computational aspects of the work.
